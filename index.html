Drowsiness Detection System
Patent PublishedNational Hackathon 4th

Real time fatigue detection pipeline using EAR, MAR, and PERCLOS metrics computed on a hybrid face detection stack combining Haar cascades and HOG-SVM. Triggers a threshold-based in-cab alarm and pushes an SOS with GPS coordinates to a fleet manager dashboard over a Flask backend. Designed for low-tech, offline-first deployment on existing commercial vehicles.

PythonOpenCVFlaskLinux
View on GitHub ↗ Patent No. 202541037313
Patent
Published · National hackathon finalist

There's a highway that passes right next to my college. For a while I kept seeing and hearing about accidents happening there at night, almost always heavy vehicles, trucks doing long distance routes. I knew most of those trucks in India are old, minimal technology, no safety systems built in.

Before I built anything I went and talked to truck drivers directly. What they told me reframed the whole problem. They weren't falling asleep because they were careless, they were deliberately pushing through the night because that's when the roads are clear and they can cover the most distance. Sleep was something they were consciously trading off. That's a very different problem than just fatigue.

The idea became a device that sits in the truck, monitors the driver using computer vision, and the moment it detects eyes closed beyond a threshold or a face completely out of frame, an alarm goes off in the cab and an SOS with location gets pushed to the fleet manager's app. No driver has to do anything. The system is watching so they don't have to fight that battle alone.

We built the model and got it working within 24 hours at a national hackathon with a team of three and placed 4th. The hardware prototype was ready but connecting the device to the software without wifi was the blocker we ran into. The project still got published as a patent, which meant a lot given how fast we moved and how real the problem was.

HSwin-OTD
ICISC 2026Oxford Co-author

Four-stage hierarchical Swin Transformer for multi-class intraocular tumor classification on ultra wide field fundus images, outperforming VGG16, ResNet50, and ViT baselines at 97.43% accuracy. Multi-layer explainability stack using Grad-CAM++ and Integrated Gradients. RAG pipeline over 278 ophthalmology papers using BioBERT embeddings and ChromaDB, generating literature-backed diagnostic reports per classification.

I was looking for a gap, a problem that mattered and that nobody was really solving well. Intraocular tumors kept coming up. They're relatively rare compared to other eye diseases which is exactly why research on them is thin, but they're also quietly deadly if missed early. That combination of underresearched and high stakes felt like exactly the kind of problem worth going after.

The challenge with medical AI though is that accuracy alone is not enough. A doctor is not going to trust a model that just says "this is a tumor" with no explanation. So the framework had to do two things at once, classify correctly and show its work.

What we built does exactly that. The model looks at ultra wide field fundus images of the eye, classifies them into tumor types with 97.43% accuracy, and then generates a full explanation of why. It highlights the specific regions of the eye it focused on to make that call, and it pulls from 278 published ophthalmology papers to back up the classification with actual medical literature. A doctor using this doesn't just get a label, they get a report they can interrogate.

That last part, grounding the AI's decision in real published research rather than just gradient maps, is what made this feel like something that could actually be used in practice and not just a research exercise. The paper caught the attention of a professor at the University of Oxford who agreed to come on as a co-author. That meant a lot, not just as validation but because it confirmed that what we built was taken seriously beyond the classroom.

CroPro — AI Crop Advisor
HOC Hackathon24 hrs

Full stack web app built in 24 hours using a Random Forest classifier trained on government soil test parameters for crop recommendation. Integrates a live weather API for location-based irrigation scheduling and a CNN-based image classification pipeline for plant disease detection, returning pesticide recommendations by active chemical composition rather than brand name.

PythonFlaskscikit-learnpandasmatplotlib
Team repo · private
Lead
Backend, ML integration, and visualisations

I grew up in a family of farmers. My grandparents farmed and I watched that world closely enough to understand what knowledge actually gets passed down and what gets lost when the next generation steps in without the same years of experience.

Modern farmers, especially younger ones stepping into it for the first time, often have access to soil test reports from the government but no clear way to act on the numbers. They know something is wrong with a plant but don't know what chemical to reach for. They're making irrigation calls based on guesswork instead of actual weather data.

CroPro connects those dots. You put in your soil values from the government test and it tells you what to grow. It reads your location's weather and tells you how much to irrigate and when. And if something looks wrong with a crop, you take a photo, upload it, and it identifies the disease and gives you the exact chemical composition you need to treat it, not just a brand name but what's actually in it and why.

It's a simple idea but the gap it fills is real. Farming knowledge used to live in people. CroPro is an attempt to make sure that knowledge doesn't disappear just because the person carrying it isn't around anymore.

Query Performance Monitor
Personal Project

REST API built with FastAPI and SQLAlchemy that intercepts query execution, parses PostgreSQL EXPLAIN ANALYZE output, and returns structured index and query structure recommendations. Tested against 50K+ row datasets with a ~30% reduction in slow query count. Built to surface what ORMs abstract away.

PythonFastAPISQLAlchemyPostgreSQLLinux
View on GitHub ↗
~30%
fewer slow queries across 50K+ row datasets

This one came from spending time in production systems at ServiceNow and wanting to understand what was actually happening at the database layer when things slowed down. Most engineers interact with databases through ORMs and never look at what the generated SQL actually does.

The monitor sits between the application and the database, captures queries as they run, parses the execution plans, and surfaces actionable recommendations. Not vague warnings, but specific things like "this column needs an index" or "this join is doing a full table scan." It cut slow query counts by around 30% on the datasets I tested it against.
